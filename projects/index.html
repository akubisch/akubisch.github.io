Plasticity vs. evolvability vs. dispersal: dealing with stress in space and time

One major current focus of eco-evolutionary research lies on the ability of species to cope with rapid changes in environmental conditions, either in space or time. When populations face such challenges, they either have to adapt to, tolerate or track these changing conditions by dispersal. Otherwise, extinction is inevitable. In this project we investigate, under which conditions it is more beneficial for species to adapt to rapid environmental changes by either (adaptive) plasticity, increasing evolvability or dispersal. The results of this project will be beneficial for projecting changes in ecosystems during impending environmental changes and may contribute to a better understanding of the role of (epi)genetic variation for the ecology and evolution of species.

Effects of changing climate and fire regimes on South-African proteaceae

Differences in the ecological niches and geographical range dynamics of species are of key importance for biodiversity research. In this project we use a large dataset on fire-adapted South-African Proteaceae species to calculate demographic response curves (i.e. niches) of 26 plant species. These niches will be combined with predicted changes in climate variables and fire regimes to assess and predict the species' responses and vulnerabilities to changing environments.

Eco-evolutionary dynamics of forest trees under climate change

Climate change is going to have several severe impacts on ecosystems. The focus of this project is to investigate these influences on the emergence of ecological and evolutionary tipping points in European tree species. In our work package we currently focus on the effects of changing fire regimes on the adaptation and survival of Mediterranean pine species. Using models informed by field observations and experiments we aim at identifying tipping points and developing management strategies that enhance adaptation and persistence of tree species in the future.

Information, Movement and Dispersal

In classical patch-matrix metapopulation models dispersal is simulated by assuming single 'jump' events of individuals from one habitat patch to another, which may be associated with some cost. This purely phenomenological approach certainly limits the reliability of metapopulation models. In this project we develop new dispersal models with a sound mechanistic foundation to increase the spatial and temporal resolution of dispersal, leading to mechanistic movement models. We will include different sources of information (genetic, social etc.) for the adequate timing and direction of dispersal and investigate influences of the landscape structure.
